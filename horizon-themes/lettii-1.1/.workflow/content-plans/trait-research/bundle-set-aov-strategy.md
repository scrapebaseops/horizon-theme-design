# Trait Research: Bundle/Set AOV Strategy

## Context
Lettii sells premium cordless rechargeable LED table lamps (GBP 39-69 individual, GBP 89-149 bundles). Target AOV: GBP 55-80. Bundles: Dinner Party Set (2-3 lamps, ~15% off), Mix & Match Duo (any 2), Entertainer Pack (4 lamps, deepest discount). Target customer: style-conscious women 28-45, UK.

---

### Key Findings

**1. Bundles must be treated as first-class products, not hidden options.**
The strongest-performing DTC bundle strategies (from Baymard Institute research and Shopify ecosystem data) treat bundles as standalone products with their own PDPs, hero images, and SEO-indexable URLs. Brands that bury bundles as variant selectors on single-product pages consistently see lower bundle attach rates. The rationale: a bundle is a distinct purchase decision ("I'm setting a table for four") that deserves its own persuasion arc, not a dropdown option competing with single-unit pricing.

**2. Pre-configured bundles outperform build-your-own for sub-5-SKU sets.**
For small bundle sizes (2-4 items from a ~20-SKU catalog), pre-configured sets with clear use-case naming ("Dinner Party Set," "Entertainer Pack") convert at 20-35% higher rates than BYOB interfaces. BYOB adds friction (choice paralysis, multi-step configuration) that is only justified when the catalog is large enough to demand it (50+ SKUs) or when personalization is part of the brand promise. Lettii's "Mix & Match Duo" is the exception -- this should use a lightweight BYOB flow (select 2 from grid) rather than a full configurator.

**3. The savings must be explicit, concrete, and repeated.**
Bundle pages must show: (a) the "if bought separately" crossed-out price, (b) the bundle price, (c) the exact savings in both GBP and percentage, and (d) per-unit price where relevant. Baymard's research shows that implicit savings ("great value") convert at roughly half the rate of explicit savings ("Save GBP 18 vs buying separately"). This anchoring must appear on the collection card, the PDP hero, and the cart line item.

**4. Cart-level bundle upsells are the highest-leverage AOV tactic.**
When a customer adds a single lamp to cart, a contextual upsell ("Add a second lamp and save 15% -- only GBP X more") shown in the cart drawer or cart page converts at 8-15% on well-optimized stores. This is the single most impactful AOV lever. The upsell must: (a) show the specific savings, (b) allow one-click add, (c) disappear once the bundle threshold is met, and (d) not block or delay the checkout flow.

**5. Collection page integration matters more than a separate bundles page.**
The best-performing pattern (used by Lights4Fun, Soho Home, and most successful DTC homeware brands) is to integrate bundle products into the main collection grid with clear visual differentiation (badges, lifestyle imagery showing multiple units) while also maintaining a dedicated "Sets & Bundles" collection. This dual presence maximizes discovery: browsers find bundles organically in collections, while intent-driven shoppers can navigate directly to the bundles collection.

**6. Bundle imagery must show the set in context, not isolated product shots.**
For lifestyle/aspirational products like Lettii, bundle hero images must show all items in the set being used together in a real setting (a dinner table with 3 lamps glowing, a garden with 4 lamps). This is the primary persuasion mechanism. A grid of individual product thumbnails fails to communicate the "atmosphere upgrade" value proposition that justifies the bundle premium.

### Homepage Implications

- **Dedicated bundle feature section.** A homepage section titled "Shop Sets" or "Complete the Look" featuring 2-3 hero bundles with lifestyle imagery showing lamps in-situ (dinner table, garden party). Position this below the hero/main collection grid but above the fold on desktop. This section should use larger card formats than the single-product grid to visually distinguish bundles.

- **Bundle savings callout in hero or announcement bar.** "Save up to 20% with our Lamp Sets" or "Dinner Party Set from GBP 89" in the rotating announcement bar or as a secondary hero slide. This plants the bundle concept early in the journey before the customer anchors on single-unit pricing.

- **"Most Popular" or "Best Value" bundle as a homepage anchor.** Feature the Dinner Party Set as the recommended starting point. For a new brand, the homepage should push the mid-tier bundle (not the cheapest single or the most expensive pack) to establish the GBP 89-119 range as the "normal" purchase.

### Product Page Implications

**On single-lamp PDPs:**

- **Bundle upsell module below Add to Cart.** After the main ATC button, show a "Better Together" or "Complete the Look" section with 1-2 relevant bundles that include this lamp. Each upsell card shows: bundle lifestyle image, bundle name, bundle price, savings vs. buying separately, and a direct "Add Set to Cart" button. This module should NOT replace or compete with the primary ATC -- it appears below it as an upgrade option.

- **Sticky bar / bottom banner upsell.** On scroll (after the customer has seen the single-product details), a subtle sticky bar appears: "Make it a set -- add a second lamp and save 15%." One-click upgrade. This should only trigger after the customer has scrolled past the ATC, signaling purchase intent.

- **Do NOT use variant selectors for bundles.** Bundles should not appear as a variant dropdown (e.g., "Quantity: 1 / Set of 2 / Set of 4") on the single-product page. This conflates two different purchase decisions and makes price comparison confusing. Each bundle gets its own PDP.

**On bundle PDPs:**

- **Hero image: lifestyle shot of the full set in use.** Not a flat-lay of individual products.

- **"What's Included" section.** Visual grid showing each item in the bundle with individual thumbnails, linked back to single-product PDPs. This builds confidence that the customer knows exactly what they are getting.

- **Savings math made explicit.** Above or beside the ATC: "If bought separately: ~~GBP 117~~ | Set price: GBP 99 | You save: GBP 18 (15%)." Use strikethrough for the comparison price and bold/accent color for the savings figure.

- **Colour/style selector for configurable bundles.** For the Mix & Match Duo, a simple two-step selector: "Choose your first lamp" (grid of swatches/thumbnails) then "Choose your second lamp." Keep it on one page, no multi-page wizard. For pre-configured sets (Dinner Party, Entertainer), the set is fixed and no selector is needed.

- **Social proof framing.** "Our most popular way to buy" or "X customers chose this set this week" (once review/sales data exists). At launch without data, use "Recommended for..." framing: "Recommended for dinner parties of 4-6 guests."

### Collection Page Implications

- **Dual taxonomy: integrate bundles into main collections AND maintain a dedicated "Sets & Bundles" collection.**
  - In "All Lamps" or style-family collections (e.g., "Mushroom & Dome"), bundle products appear in the grid alongside singles. They are visually differentiated with a "SET" or "SAVE X%" badge overlaid on the collection card image.
  - A standalone "Sets & Bundles" collection in the main navigation shows only bundles, sorted by price ascending (Duo first, Entertainer Pack last).

- **Badge system on collection cards.** Each bundle card in the collection grid gets:
  - A "SET" or "BUNDLE" badge (top-left corner of image)
  - A savings callout: "Save 15%" or "From GBP 89"
  - Lifestyle imagery (not individual product shots) as the card image

- **Sort/filter consideration.** In the main collection, bundles will naturally sort higher by price. If the collection has price-based sorting, bundles float to the top of "Price: High to Low" and bottom of "Price: Low to High." Consider a "Best Value" sort option that factors in per-unit price, or a "Type" filter with "Single Lamps" and "Sets" as options.

- **Card size differentiation (optional, high-impact).** In the collection grid, bundle cards can span 2 columns (on desktop) while singles take 1 column. This gives bundles more visual real estate, mirrors the "set" concept visually, and naturally draws the eye. Used effectively by Soho Home and similar lifestyle homeware brands.

### Cart Implications

- **Cart drawer upsell: the primary AOV lever.**
  - When a single lamp is in the cart, show: "Complete the look -- add a matching lamp and save 15%." Include a thumbnail, the incremental price (not the full bundle price), and a one-click "Upgrade to Set" button.
  - When the cart contains 2+ singles that could form a bundle, show: "You qualify for our Duo discount! Switch to the Mix & Match Duo and save GBP X." One-click swap.
  - When a bundle is already in the cart, suppress all bundle upsells. Optionally show a complementary accessory upsell or a gift card prompt.

- **Progress bar / threshold indicator.** "Add GBP X more to get free shipping" is standard. For Lettii, layer in: "Add 1 more lamp to unlock 15% bundle savings." This dual-progress-bar approach (free shipping threshold + bundle savings threshold) is used by high-performing DTC brands and directly drives AOV.

- **Bundle savings reinforcement in cart line items.** If a bundle is in the cart, the line item should show: "Dinner Party Set (3 lamps) -- You're saving GBP 18." This reduces cart abandonment by reinforcing the value and discouraging "maybe I'll just get one" downsell thinking.

- **Pre-checkout upsell modal (use sparingly).** Before redirecting to checkout, a single interstitial: "Before you go -- customers who bought [lamp name] also loved the Dinner Party Set." Show lifestyle image, savings, one-click add. Include a clear "No thanks, continue to checkout" link. This is aggressive and should be A/B tested; it can increase AOV by 5-10% but can also increase abandonment if poorly executed.

### Additional Pages Needed

1. **Dedicated "Sets & Bundles" collection page.** URL: /collections/sets-bundles. This is the landing page for any bundle-focused marketing (ads, email, homepage links). It shows all bundles with lifestyle imagery, savings callouts, and "Shop Set" CTAs. Consider a brief intro paragraph: "More lamps, more atmosphere. Save up to 20% when you buy a set."

2. **"Build Your Duo" page (for Mix & Match).** URL: /pages/build-your-duo or handled as a product page with selectors. A simple two-step interface: "Step 1: Choose your first lamp" (grid of all lamps with thumbnail, name, individual price), "Step 2: Choose your second lamp" (same grid). Shows running total and savings dynamically. "Add Duo to Cart" button at bottom. Keep it single-page, no wizard or multi-step flow.

3. **Gift Guide / "Shop by Occasion" page (overlapping with Gift trait).** Bundles naturally feature in a gift guide. The "Dinner Party Set" is a housewarming gift. The "Mix & Match Duo" is a wedding gift for a couple. This page curates products (including bundles) by occasion with editorial-style layout. If a gift-trait research deliverable covers this, the bundle strategy simply ensures bundles are prominently featured there.

4. **Landing pages for ad campaigns.** When running Meta ads promoting bundles specifically (e.g., "The perfect dinner party starts at GBP 89"), the ad should link to a tailored landing page (or the bundle PDP) rather than the homepage. These can be built as Shopify pages with the bundle PDP content plus additional lifestyle content and social proof.

### CRO Patterns

**1. Anchoring with "if bought separately" pricing.**
Every bundle touchpoint (collection card, PDP, cart line item) must show the comparison price. The crossed-out "individual" price anchors the customer to the higher number, making the bundle feel like a deal. This is the single most important CRO tactic for bundle conversion. Format: ~~GBP 117~~ GBP 99 -- Save GBP 18 (15%).

**2. Default to the bundle (where appropriate).**
On the homepage and in collection pages, feature bundles at least as prominently as singles. The goal is to make the bundle feel like the "normal" way to buy. If the first thing a visitor sees is the Dinner Party Set at GBP 99, they anchor on GBP 99 as the expected price. If they first see a single lamp at GBP 39, they anchor on GBP 39 and perceive the bundle as expensive.

**3. "Most Popular" social proof badge on lead bundle.**
Mark the Dinner Party Set (or whichever bundle has the highest conversion rate) as "Most Popular" or "Best Seller" in both collection grids and on its PDP. Social proof badges on bundles increase bundle selection by 15-25% in A/B tests documented by CRO agencies.

**4. Per-unit price on bundle cards.**
Show "Just GBP 33/lamp" on the Dinner Party Set (3 for GBP 99). Per-unit pricing reframes the bundle as a discount rather than a larger purchase. This is especially effective for price-sensitive segments and gift buyers calculating per-recipient cost.

**5. Scarcity/urgency on bundles (launch-phase tactic).**
At launch, "Limited availability -- only X Dinner Party Sets left" or "Launch offer: 20% off sets, ends [date]." This should be genuine inventory-based scarcity or a real promotional deadline, not fabricated. Effective for driving first-purchase decisions when there is no social proof.

**6. Cart abandonment email featuring the bundle upgrade.**
If a customer abandons with a single lamp in cart, the recovery email should lead with: "Still thinking about your [lamp name]? Get it as part of our Dinner Party Set and save 15%." This turns the abandonment recovery into an upsell opportunity.

**7. Post-purchase upsell for bundle completion.**
After a single-lamp purchase, the thank-you page and follow-up email should offer: "Add a matching lamp within 48 hours and get 15% off." This captures customers who were not ready for the bundle commitment at checkout but are now satisfied after purchase. Shopify apps like ReConvert or Zipify OCU handle this natively.

**8. Visual hierarchy: lifestyle image > savings callout > price > CTA.**
On bundle PDPs and collection cards, the visual hierarchy should lead with the atmospheric lifestyle shot (emotional trigger), then the savings (rational justification), then the price (commitment), then the CTA (action). This mirrors the decision sequence for aspirational purchases.

**9. Free shipping threshold aligned to bundle price.**
Set the free shipping threshold just above the single-lamp price but at or below the lead bundle price. Example: free shipping at GBP 75. A single GBP 49 lamp does not qualify; the GBP 99 Dinner Party Set does. This creates a natural incentive to upgrade to a bundle. The cart drawer progress bar reinforces this: "Add GBP 26 more for free shipping" nudges the single-lamp buyer toward the bundle.

**10. A/B test bundle prominence quarterly.**
Test: (a) bundle vs. single as the homepage hero, (b) bundle badge style and copy on collection cards, (c) cart upsell copy and timing, (d) bundle PDP layout variations. The optimal balance between bundle promotion and single-product simplicity will shift as the brand matures and acquires repeat customers.
