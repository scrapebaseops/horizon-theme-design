# Store Trait: Dual Audience — D2C Consumer + B2B Trade/Hospitality

## What This Trait Is

This trait applies when a store's primary audience is individual consumers (D2C) but a meaningful secondary audience exists of trade/business buyers who purchase in volume — typically 10–50+ units at a time. Common B2B segments include hospitality (restaurants, bars, hotels), event professionals (wedding planners, event stylists), and commercial buyers (offices, retail fit-outs).

The core UX challenge: serve the B2B buyer without confusing, cluttering, or diluting the consumer shopping experience.

## When This Trait Applies

- The brand brief identifies a B2B or trade audience alongside consumer sales
- The product has clear commercial/hospitality applications (lighting, tableware, furniture, textiles, fragrance)
- Volume purchasing (10+ units) is a realistic and desired buying pattern
- The brand wants to capture B2B leads or orders through the same domain

## Key Findings

### The "Quiet Door" Principle

The dominant pattern among successful DTC-first brands that also serve B2B is what can be called the "quiet door" approach: the consumer experience is the default, uncluttered experience. B2B entry points exist but are deliberately understated — visible to anyone looking for them but never competing with consumer CTAs for attention.

This is how brands like Soho Home, Zafferano, and numerous Shopify-based DTC brands handle the split. The rationale is straightforward:

1. **Consumer visitors vastly outnumber B2B visitors** (typically 90–95% vs 5–10% of traffic). The default experience must serve the majority.
2. **B2B buyers are high-intent and will seek out trade options.** A restaurant owner looking to buy 30 lamps will actively look for "trade," "wholesale," or "hospitality" links. They do not need to be marketed to in the same way as a casual browser.
3. **B2B messaging (volume pricing, MOQs, lead times, account applications) actively harms consumer conversion** if shown prominently. A consumer seeing "minimum order 10 units" on a product page will be confused or deterred.

### Separate Experiences, Same Domain

The established best practice is: same Shopify store, same domain, but separate landing pages for B2B audiences. A subdomain (trade.brand.com) is unnecessary and creates SEO fragmentation, maintenance overhead, and a disconnected brand experience. Instead, B2B content lives on dedicated pages within the main store, reached via:

- Footer navigation (most common)
- A secondary top-nav link (for brands where B2B is a significant revenue share)
- Direct links from Google Ads campaigns targeting trade keywords
- Email outreach with direct links to the trade page

### Soho Home's Trade/Contract Model

Soho Home (the homeware arm of Soho House) uses a "Trade & Contract" section accessible from both the top navigation and footer. Their approach:

- **Top navigation**: A simple "Trade" link in the top nav bar, styled identically to other nav items (not highlighted or prominent)
- **Trade landing page**: Contains a brief value proposition ("Furnish your project with Soho Home"), a trade account application form, benefits of a trade account (percentage discount, dedicated account manager, access to full range), and imagery showing products in commercial settings (hotels, restaurants, members' clubs)
- **Product pages remain consumer-focused**: No trade pricing or volume CTAs on standard product pages. Trade customers are expected to browse the same catalog but enquire/order through their trade account or account manager
- **Separate login**: Trade account holders log in to see trade pricing; regular visitors see consumer pricing. This keeps a single product catalog with dual pricing tiers

### Zafferano's Hospitality Approach

Zafferano (premium cordless lamp brand, directly comparable to Lettii) serves hospitality prominently:

- **Dedicated "Hospitality" section** in main navigation, equal prominence to consumer collections
- **Hospitality landing page** features: commercial-setting imagery (restaurants, hotels, bars), a curated selection of their most popular hospitality models, bulk pricing information, and a contact/enquiry form
- **Case studies/gallery**: Photos of real restaurants and hotels using their lamps, with venue names — this is powerful social proof for B2B buyers
- **Product pages include both audiences**: Their product pages show the lamp in both home and commercial settings, with a "Request a quote" or "Hospitality enquiry" CTA alongside the consumer "Add to Cart"

Zafferano can do this because their brand positioning is already hospitality-adjacent. For Lettii, which leads with consumer/lifestyle positioning, a lighter B2B treatment is more appropriate.

### "For Weddings & Events" as a Distinct Concept

Research across DTC brands in lighting, tableware, and decor consistently shows that "Weddings & Events" functions as a separate concept from B2B/trade, even though the buyer may overlap:

- **B2B/Trade** = ongoing commercial relationship, repeat orders, trade account, volume pricing
- **Weddings & Events** = project-based, often one-time large purchase, emotionally driven, deadline-sensitive

A wedding planner buying 40 lamps for a venue responds to different messaging than a restaurant owner. The wedding buyer wants:
- Imagery of lamps at weddings, receptions, outdoor ceremonies
- Assurance of delivery timelines relative to the event date
- Potential rental or hire enquiry options (even if not offered, acknowledging the question builds trust)
- Bulk pricing but framed as "event packages" rather than "wholesale"
- Coordination support ("Can I get 40 units in the same colour by June 15?")

**Recommendation: Create two separate landing pages** — one for "Restaurants & Hospitality" (trade/wholesale focus) and one for "Weddings & Events" (project/occasion focus). They can share a similar structure but with different imagery, messaging, and CTAs.

## Structural Impact

### Homepage Implications

The homepage should remain **100% consumer-focused**. Do not add B2B messaging, trade banners, or hospitality CTAs to the homepage. The reasoning:

- 90%+ of homepage visitors are consumers (organic, social, direct)
- B2B buyers arriving from Google Ads will land on dedicated B2B pages, not the homepage
- Any B2B messaging on the homepage creates cognitive load for consumer visitors ("Is this a wholesale site? Can I buy just one?")

The **one exception**: if Lettii establishes significant B2B traction, a brief social proof element like "Trusted by 200+ restaurants and venues across the UK" can appear on the homepage as a trust signal. This benefits both audiences — consumers see it as quality validation, B2B buyers see it as category credibility. But this should be a single line within a trust bar or social proof section, not a dedicated B2B block.

Additionally, the "Shop by Setting" section on the homepage (if included) should list "Restaurants & Bars" as a setting alongside "Dining & Entertaining," "Bedroom & Bedside," and "Garden & Outdoor." This serves as a subtle B2B entry point — a restaurant owner browsing casually sees their setting acknowledged and can click through to the relevant collection, which in turn links to the full hospitality page.

### Product Page Implications

Product pages should be **primarily consumer-focused** with a single, understated B2B entry point:

- **Do NOT show volume/wholesale pricing on product pages.** This confuses consumers and anchors them to lower per-unit prices they cannot access.
- **Do NOT add a "Trade Account" CTA competing with "Add to Cart."** This creates decision paralysis.
- **DO add a single line below the main CTA area**: something like "Ordering for a restaurant, hotel, or event? [Get a quote]" — linked to the hospitality enquiry page. This should be visually secondary (smaller text, muted colour, no button — just a text link).
- **DO include hospitality/commercial lifestyle imagery** in the product gallery alongside home settings. A lamp shown on a restaurant table communicates versatility to consumers ("this looks like what I see in restaurants") while signalling suitability to B2B browsers.
- **Specs section**: Include a brief mention of commercial suitability ("IP54 rated — suitable for commercial and outdoor hospitality use. Bulk and trade enquiries welcome.") within the specifications or FAQ accordion.

### Collection Page Implications

- **"Restaurants & Bars" collection**: This should exist as a standard Shopify collection under "Shop by Setting." It shows the same lamps but with hospitality-focused imagery and descriptions. At the top or bottom of this collection page, include a banner: "Outfitting a venue? See our hospitality packages and volume pricing" linking to the full B2B landing page.
- **Other collection pages** (Dining & Entertaining, Garden & Outdoor, etc.) should remain purely consumer-focused with no B2B messaging.
- **Bundle collection**: The standard bundles (Dinner Party Set, Entertainer Pack) are consumer bundles. Do not mix B2B volume bundles into the same collection. B2B volume pricing lives on the hospitality page only.

### Cart & Checkout Implications

- Keep cart and checkout 100% consumer-focused. No trade pricing, no wholesale logic.
- B2B orders will be handled via the enquiry form (off-platform negotiation → manual order or draft order in Shopify). This is standard practice for DTC brands with B2B — trying to automate B2B pricing in a consumer Shopify checkout creates complexity without payoff until B2B volume is very high.

## Additional Pages Needed

### 1. "For Restaurants & Hospitality" Landing Page

**URL**: `/pages/hospitality` or `/pages/for-restaurants`

**Purpose**: Convert B2B visitors arriving from Google Ads ("restaurant table lamps bulk," "cordless lamps for restaurants wholesale") or from footer/nav links on the main site.

**Content structure**:

1. **Hero section**: Full-width image of lamps in a restaurant/bar setting. Headline: "Cordless table lighting for restaurants, bars, and hotels." Subheadline: "Premium rechargeable lamps. Volume pricing. Dedicated support."
2. **Social proof bar**: "Trusted by [X] venues across the UK" (populate once traction exists; at launch, use "Designed for hospitality. Built to last.")
3. **Product showcase**: 4–6 of the most commercially suitable lamp designs, shown in hospitality settings. Each with a brief description focused on commercial benefits (battery life per shift, charge time, durability, IP54 rating).
4. **Volume pricing table**: Simple tier table showing per-unit pricing at different quantities:
   - 1–9 units: Standard retail price
   - 10–24 units: X% discount
   - 25–49 units: X% discount
   - 50+ units: Contact for custom pricing

   (Exact discounts to be determined by margin analysis. The table itself is the key UX element — B2B buyers expect to see volume pricing clearly.)
5. **Commercial benefits section**: 3–4 blocks highlighting key B2B value props:
   - "12+ hours per charge — lasts a full dinner service"
   - "IP54 waterproof — terrace and outdoor dining ready"
   - "USB-C charging — stack and charge overnight, ready by service"
   - "No fire risk — insurance-friendly alternative to candles"
6. **Case studies / venue gallery**: Photos of lamps in real venues (initially AI-generated settings; replaced with real venue photos as traction grows). If possible, include a quote from a venue operator.
7. **Enquiry form**: Name, business name, email, phone, estimated quantity needed, message. Keep it short — 5–6 fields maximum. The goal is to capture the lead, not complete the sale on-page.
8. **FAQ section**: Common B2B questions — lead times, minimum order quantities, returns policy for bulk orders, custom branding options (if applicable), sample orders.

### 2. "For Weddings & Events" Landing Page

**URL**: `/pages/weddings` or `/pages/weddings-and-events`

**Purpose**: Capture wedding planners, event stylists, and individuals planning large events who need multiple lamps.

**Content structure**:

1. **Hero section**: Lamps in a wedding reception or outdoor event setting. Headline: "Light your big day." Subheadline: "Cordless table lamps for weddings, receptions, and celebrations."
2. **Gallery**: 4–6 images showing lamps across different wedding/event scenarios — outdoor ceremonies, reception tables, cocktail bars, garden parties, marquees.
3. **Event packages**: Present volume options as "event packages" rather than wholesale tiers:
   - "Intimate gathering" (6–10 lamps)
   - "Reception" (15–25 lamps)
   - "Grand event" (30+ lamps)
   - Each with a suggested lamp style and per-unit pricing
4. **Practical information**: Lead time guarantees, delivery scheduling, what happens if you need more/fewer, coordination support available.
5. **Enquiry form**: Similar to hospitality but with event-specific fields — event date, venue location, estimated number of tables. This helps Lettii prioritise urgent enquiries.
6. **Inspirational content**: Link to blog posts like "Wedding table lighting ideas" or "How to style outdoor reception tables" — serves both conversion and SEO.

### 3. Wholesale/Trade Enquiry Confirmation Page

A simple thank-you page after form submission confirming the enquiry was received, expected response time (within 24–48 hours), and a prompt to browse the consumer collection while they wait.

## Navigation Treatment

### Primary Recommendation: Footer + Contextual Links

**Footer navigation**: Add a "Business" or "Trade" section in the footer with links to:
- For Restaurants & Hospitality
- For Weddings & Events
- Wholesale Enquiries (can link to the same hospitality page if separate enquiry page is unnecessary)

**Top navigation**: Do NOT add B2B links to the primary top navigation at launch. The top nav should be reserved for consumer collections (Shop All, Shop by Style, Shop by Setting, Bundles, Our Story). Reasons:
- Top nav space is extremely limited on mobile (hamburger menu)
- Adding "Trade" or "Hospitality" to top nav signals a split-focus brand, which undermines consumer confidence at launch
- B2B traffic will arrive via Google Ads directly on B2B landing pages, not via homepage navigation

**Exception**: Once B2B becomes a significant revenue channel (e.g., 15%+ of revenue), adding a top-nav "For Business" dropdown becomes appropriate. This is a phase-2 decision.

**Contextual links**: The "Restaurants & Bars" collection page (under Shop by Setting) should include a prominent banner linking to the hospitality page. This is the most natural B2B entry point for someone browsing the consumer site who happens to be a venue operator.

### Mobile Navigation

In the mobile hamburger menu, B2B pages should appear at the bottom of the menu, below all consumer categories. They should be grouped under a "For Business" or "For Professionals" heading. This keeps them accessible without displacing consumer navigation.

## CRO Patterns

### 1. Lead Capture Over Direct Sale

For B2B, the conversion goal is **lead capture (enquiry form submission)**, not add-to-cart. B2B orders in this price range and volume are consultative — the buyer wants to discuss quantities, delivery timelines, potential discounts, and sometimes see samples before committing. Trying to force a standard e-commerce checkout for a 30-unit order creates friction and reduces conversion.

**Pattern**: Every B2B page should have a single, clear CTA: "Get a Quote" or "Request Trade Pricing." The enquiry form should be embedded on the page (not on a separate page requiring an extra click). Place the form both mid-page and at the bottom.

### 2. "Sample Order" as Low-Commitment Entry

Offer B2B prospects the option to "Order a sample" at the retail price — they can buy 1–2 lamps through the normal consumer checkout to evaluate quality before committing to a bulk order. Mention this explicitly on the hospitality page: "Want to try before you commit? Order a sample lamp at our standard price."

This converts hesitant B2B visitors into paying customers immediately, with the bulk order as a follow-up. It is the single most effective B2B conversion tactic for product-based DTC brands.

### 3. Separate Google Ads Landing Pages

B2B traffic from Google Ads (searching "cordless lamps for restaurants wholesale" or "rechargeable table lamps bulk buy") should land directly on the hospitality page — never on the homepage or a consumer product page. The hospitality landing page must be self-contained: it should convey the full value proposition without requiring the visitor to navigate elsewhere.

**Pattern**: Ensure the hospitality page has its own meta title and description optimized for B2B keywords. Use it as the landing page for B2B-targeted Google Ads campaigns.

### 4. Social Proof Separation

B2B buyers and consumers respond to different social proof:
- **Consumers** want: star ratings, individual customer reviews, lifestyle imagery, "X people bought this today"
- **B2B buyers** want: venue names/logos, number of venues served, commercial durability proof, professional endorsements

On the hospitality page, prioritize commercial social proof. "Used by [venue name]" with a photo is more persuasive to a restaurant owner than 500 five-star consumer reviews.

### 5. Urgency Without Pressure

For B2B landing pages, avoid consumer-style urgency tactics (countdown timers, "low stock" alerts). Instead, use:
- "Order by [date] for delivery before the summer season"
- "Free delivery on trade orders over [threshold]"
- Seasonal relevance: "Spring/summer is peak terrace season — plan your table lighting now"

### 6. Trust Signals Specific to B2B

B2B buyers have different trust concerns than consumers. On trade/hospitality pages, emphasise:
- UK-based company (important for B2B buyers concerned about warranty/support)
- Dedicated trade support contact (email or phone)
- Clear returns/warranty policy for bulk orders
- CE marking and relevant safety certifications (B2B buyers in hospitality must verify compliance)
- IP54 rating with specific explanation of what it means for commercial outdoor use

### 7. Follow-Up Speed Indication

On the enquiry form, state the expected response time: "We respond to all trade enquiries within 24 hours." B2B buyers often contact multiple suppliers simultaneously — the fastest to respond has a significant conversion advantage.

## Trust Signals That Matter

**For B2B specifically** (in addition to standard consumer trust signals):
- "UK-based company with UK customer support"
- Specific certifications and safety ratings (CE, IP54)
- "Trusted by [X] venues" (once established)
- Named trade contact or dedicated account manager (even if it is just the founder at launch)
- Clear volume pricing (opacity on pricing is the #1 B2B conversion killer)
- Photos of product in commercial settings (not just home settings)
- Lead time transparency
- Sample order option

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Mixing B2B and consumer messaging on the homepage.** This confuses both audiences. Keep the homepage consumer-only.
2. **Showing wholesale/volume pricing on standard product pages.** Consumers see a lower per-unit price they cannot access and feel excluded or confused.
3. **Requiring a trade account before showing any pricing.** Some gating is expected for exact trade pricing, but showing approximate volume tiers openly (on the hospitality page) converts significantly better than "apply for a trade account to see pricing."
4. **Creating a completely separate subdomain or Shopify store for B2B.** This fragments SEO, doubles maintenance, and creates a disconnected brand experience. One store, separate pages.
5. **Neglecting mobile optimization for B2B pages.** A common assumption is that B2B buyers only browse on desktop. In reality, many venue operators and event planners discover suppliers on mobile and return on desktop to complete enquiries. B2B pages must be fully mobile-responsive.
6. **Making the enquiry form too long.** Every additional form field reduces completion rates. Name, email, business name, quantity estimate, and a free-text message field is sufficient. Do not ask for full address, company registration number, or other details at the enquiry stage.
7. **Forgetting to connect B2B landing pages to the consumer catalog.** B2B visitors should be able to browse the full product range. Include links to product pages and collections from the hospitality page so trade buyers can see the full range, specs, and imagery.
8. **Treating "Weddings & Events" identically to "Trade/Wholesale."** These audiences have different emotional drivers, timelines, and expectations. Separate pages with tailored messaging convert better than a single generic "bulk order" page.
